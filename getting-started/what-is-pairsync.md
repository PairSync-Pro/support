# What is PairSync?

## The Simple Explanation

Imagine you're watching gas prices at two stations across the street from each other. Most days, they charge about the same - maybe $3.50 per gallon. But one day, Station A suddenly jumps to $4.00 while Station B stays at $3.50.

What happens? Drivers flock to Station B. Eventually, Station A must lower its price to compete, or Station B might raise theirs. The prices "snap back" together because they're competing for the same customers.

**Pairs trading works the same way with stocks.** When two related companies temporarily diverge in price, PairSync helps you spot these patterns so you can learn about mean-reversion strategies.

## What Makes PairSync Different?

### Traditional Investing vs. Pairs Trading

**Traditional Stock Picking:**
- Buy stocks you think will go up
- You profit only if the market goes up
- Exposed to overall market risk
- "Buy low, sell high"

**Pairs Trading (PairSync's Focus):**
- Simultaneously look at two related stocks
- Profit potential when they converge (regardless of market direction)
- **Market-neutral** strategy (reduces broad market risk)
- "Buy the laggard, sell the leader" (or watch patterns without trading)

### Example Scenario

**Traditional Approach:**  
"I think Tesla will go up, so I'll buy it."  
→ If the whole market crashes, you lose money even if Tesla is a good company.

**Pairs Trading Approach (Educational):**  
"Tesla and Rivian usually move together (cointegrated). Tesla just jumped 10% while Rivian stayed flat. Historically, they tend to converge. Let me study this pattern."  
→ Whether the market goes up or down, you're learning about the *relationship* between two assets, not just directional bets.

## Core Concepts You'll Learn

### 1. **Cointegration**
Two assets are cointegrated if they maintain a stable long-term relationship, even if they wander apart temporarily. Think of two friends walking together - they might drift apart briefly, but they stay together over the long haul.

[Learn More: Cointegration Explained →](../education/cointegration-explained.md)

### 2. **Mean Reversion**
When a pair's price spread deviates from its historical average, it tends to "revert" back to the mean over time. Like a rubber band stretching and snapping back.

[Learn More: Mean Reversion Concepts →](../education/mean-reversion-concepts.md)

### 3. **Z-Score**
A statistical measure of how far the current spread is from its average, measured in standard deviations. A Z-Score of +2.0 means the spread is 2 standard deviations above normal.

[Learn More: Understanding Z-Score →](../education/understanding-zscore.md)

### 4. **Market Neutrality**
A strategy that aims to profit from relative price movements rather than absolute price changes. Reduces sensitivity to overall market direction.

[Learn More: Statistical Arbitrage Basics →](../education/statistical-arbitrage-basics.md)
